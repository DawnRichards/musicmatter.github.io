Classical Music Vs technology

Putting a Piece of Classical Music Online

When someone writes a 3.30-minute song in their bedroom they have a few choices of how to get it onto the internet. They can put it on YouTube (probably the easiest), Soundcloud, Bandcamp and many other streaming services. If they market this song well and then add more to go with it then they could end up earning some money from it.

However, if someone writes a contemporary classical piece that is 5 minutes long there are fewer places online that will accommodate it, and even then, it may not get the help from the algorithm of the site if it is longer than a normal length pop song. From what I have experienced the best place to put a classical style piece is YouTube where watchers are more used to watching longer videos.

If you look on Spotify there is a section for classical music, but the number of people following those playlists is between 100,000 to 200,000, however, if you look at the playlists of pop songs there are 300,000 to 600,000 people following them. Some of these even have millions of people following them e.g. “Songs to Sing in the Shower”. This is a prime example that although you can put classical music on these platforms they simply don’t have the audience of people who want to listen to this kind of music. This is fair enough, because that is not what their main interest is, but where do we, as classical musicians put our music?

After a bit of searching I found a few sites that allow you to stream classical music for free (with ads obviously). One of them is OperaVision who, as the name suggest, mainly focus on operas which seem to have been livestreamed and then imbedded onto the website. They have managed to make the website very friendly to new-comers to opera with a link “New To Opera?” with a bunch of information behind that including an explanation of “Opera for Beginners”. This is a good place for new listeners to get to grips with opera, but it is still not the Spotify of the classical world.

The only website I found completely free, with lots of choice of music was AccuRadio. They have a very wide range of music, but more importantly to us, there is a fair amount of classical music and a few modern classical pieces. I feel like this website is trying to be the next Spotify which, I think, is not a great business model as there are so many big companies crowding the market already. However, it does seem to be fairly easy to put music up onto AccuRadio so could be a good option for modern classical composers.

Another site I liked the look of was Primephonic. This was not free but after a 30 day free trial it is £14.99 a month which is a pretty good price for an all classical music streaming service. You can also buy downloads of your favourite piece or album, quite like iTunes. The only problem with this is that it seems that to be able to get music onto the site you have to be connected to a label, which is fine if you’re with one but for the amateur composer it will be harder.

I think Soundcloud could be a great place for amateurs to put up their classical style pieces. It is a place full of new music with, what seems to me to be, very little bias on the shorter pop songs, except the listener’s attention spans which will always be a problem for a composer who writes anything more than 3 minutes (but that is a different conversation to have).

In conclusion, there are places that you can put your music online, but you have to make sure that you choose the right one for your style of writing. After writing this I think Soundcloud is a good place to start for a classical composer, but that is up to you, the writer, to decide for yourself.
